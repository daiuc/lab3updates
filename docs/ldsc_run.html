Objective 1: estimate each gene’s total cis heritability

A given gene’s total heritability can be estimated by first estimate average per SNP heritability for each annotation category \(\bar{\tau_c}\). Then A given gene’s total heritability can be computed by multiplying number of all qualifying SNPs in cis Modified ldsc codes to output \(\tau_c\) of each category as well as overlapping SNPs.

\[h^2_g(total) = \sum_C{M_c \bar{\tau_c}}\]

To compute \(M_c\) and \(\bar{\tau_c}\), I made these modifications in the original scripts of LD SCore v1.0.1:

  • Output number of SNPs for each category, essentially an inner join between cis-eQTL summary stats SNPs and LD score SNPs from each of the 53 category (including base, ie. just SNPs).

  • This involves modifying the functions of:

    • annot_parser, annot functions in the parse.py script
    • _read_annot function in the sumstats.py script
    • _overlap_output method in the regression.py script

  • With the modification, ldsc will now output correct intersected M_annot or number of cis-eQTL SNPs for each category

  • Code for computing coefficient is not modified, since it already only take in intersection between ld score SNPs and cis-eQTL SNPs

Is cis genetic heritability related to regulatory landscape, functional intolerance and constraints, or gene length?

To answer this question, I investigated the following metrics:

EDS

EDS, or enhancer-domain score, reflects the total size and redundancy of a gene’s non-coding regulatory architecture. A higher EDS suggests a gene has higher non-coding regulatory interactions. It was noted to be negatively correlated with cis eQTL (Wang et al. 2020). EDS is computed by first constructing enhancer domains for each gene from enhancer predictions from various data sources. For each enhancer domain per gene, domain size and redundancy of TF motifs are the main determinants of the final EDS value.

In my analysis, EDS data is downloaded from Wang et al. 2020 paper.


RVIS

RVIS (Residual Variation Intolerance Score) is designed to rank genes in terms of whether they have more or less common functional genetic variation relative to the genome wide expectation given the amount of apparently neutral variation the gene has. A gene with a positive score has more common functional variation, and a gene with a negative score has less and is referred to as “intolerant”. By convention we rank all genes in order from most intolerant to least. As an example, a gene such as ATP1A3 has a RVIS score of -1.53 and a percentile of 3.37%, meaning it is amongst the 3.37% most intolerant of human genes. A negative score indicates a gene has less than expected amount of functional variants, thus may be under purifying selection, while a positive score indicates higher than expected amount of functional variants, thus may be under balanced or positive selection.

RVIS score in this analysis is downloaded from the Wang et al. 2020 paper.

pLI

pLI (probability of being loss-of-function intolerant) reflects the tolerance of a gene to the loss of function mutations. A higher value of pLI indicates a gene’s function is more likely to be abrogated under a loss-of-function mutation. It is frequently used to prioritize candidate genes when analyzing whole exome or whole genome data. Note that pLI specifically measures the impact of loss of function variants, such as missense, frame shift, early stop codon, etc.

gene length

I filtered protein coding genes and computed gene length from Gencode hg38 V31 GTF file.

max.abs.slope

I downloaded cis-eQTL significant SNP-gene pair dataset from GTEx V8 analysis. Data from 49 single tissue specific datasets are merged. For this metric as well as the N_QTL metric, all significant SNPs are subject to MAF filtering consistent with heritability estimate (0.05 < MAF < 0.95). Maximum effect size is summarised for each gene using the regression slope (slope) column in the *.eqtl_signifpairs.txt.gz data files. The slope values are first taken as absolute value before max summarising by gene.

GTEx V8 N_QTL

N_QTL refers to the number of significant cis-eQTLs per gene.

Interprete result

I performed correlation test between \(h^2\) and all the other metrics. EDS, gene length, and N_eQTLs showed the strongest correlation. All are inversely correlated with \(h^2\).

  • EDS was previously noted to negatively correlate with cis-eQTLs. One explanation is that mammalian genes have evolved a robustness to cope with regulatory genetic variation (Wang et al.). Thus genes with high EDS would be better explained by genomewide variants, rather than cis variants. This perhaps explains why higher EDS genes might have lower cis \(h^2\).

  • gene length:

    • possibly because longer genes do not necessarily contributes to more qualifying SNPs, as a result longer genes are penalized for heritability?
    • should we restrict to length of CDS only?

  • N_QTLs: strange that it’s negatively correlated.

    • Perhaps due to many more SNPs/eQTLs in the GTEx dataset relative to SNPs used for \(h^2\) (eQTLgen x EUR hapmap3)
    • eQTLgen only has blood samples, but GTEx more diverse

  • RVIS is positively correlated. Since a higher positive RVIS score means that there are likely more functional variants than expected (possible positive selection). Also from previous plot, it suggests that higher cis heritability may originate from the collective contribution of larger amount of common variants. From this standpoint, it makes sense that the two metrics should move in the same direction.
